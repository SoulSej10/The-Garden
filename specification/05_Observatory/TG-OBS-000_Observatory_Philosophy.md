# Purpose

This document establishes the philosophy of the Observatory.

The Observatory is the player's primary means of understanding, exploring, and studying the living world.

It is not merely a user interface.

It is the bridge between an unimaginably complex simulation and human understanding.

---

# Core Principle

**The Observatory exists to reveal, never to control.**

The simulation creates reality.

The Story System recognizes meaning.

The Observatory makes that meaning discoverable.

Its purpose is understanding rather than management.

---

# Design Philosophy

The Garden is not a game about issuing commands.

It is a game about observation.

The Observatory encourages players to:

Watch.

Question.

Compare.

Investigate.

Learn.

Reflect.

Every interface should deepen understanding of the world rather than distract from it.

---

# The Observatory as a Place

The Observatory is a conceptual space rather than a single screen.

It includes every tool that allows the player to examine the simulation.

Examples include:

Maps.

Historical archives.

Citizen records.

Civilization chronicles.

Scientific discoveries.

Timelines.

Environmental analysis.

Relationship networks.

Every feature shares the same purpose:

Helping the player understand the living world.

---

# Observation Before Interaction

Players should first understand.

Only then decide whether to intervene.

The Observatory encourages informed participation instead of impulsive action.

Knowledge becomes the foundation of meaningful decisions.

---

# Respect for the Simulation

The Observatory never invents information.

It presents what the simulation knows.

When uncertainty exists, uncertainty is presented honestly.

Missing knowledge remains missing until discovered.

The interface should never pretend certainty where none exists.

---

# Curiosity as Navigation

The Observatory should answer questions naturally.

Examples include:

Why did this civilization become prosperous?

How did this family become influential?

Why did this ecosystem collapse?

What caused this migration?

What happened here two hundred years ago?

Every answer should encourage new questions.

---

# Progressive Understanding

The player should never be overwhelmed.

Information is revealed progressively.

Surface observations lead to deeper investigation.

Simple questions uncover complex relationships.

The Observatory supports both casual exploration and scholarly analysis.

---

# Neutral Presentation

The Observatory does not judge history.

It does not celebrate.

It does not condemn.

It records.

Presents.

Connects.

The player forms their own interpretations.

---

# Transparency

Whenever possible, conclusions should be traceable.

If a city prospered because of trade, the player should be able to follow that chain of evidence.

Understanding should emerge from observable relationships rather than hidden calculations.

---

# Living Knowledge

The Observatory is never static.

As the world changes:

Maps evolve.

Histories expand.

Citizens age.

Civilizations transform.

Relationships shift.

Knowledge grows alongside the simulation.

---

# State Variables

The Observatory observes and presents:

Historical Knowledge.

Scientific Knowledge.

Environmental Knowledge.

Civilizational Knowledge.

Relationship Networks.

World Memory.

Player Discoveries.

Narrative Connections.

These variables describe what is knowable rather than altering the simulation itself.

# Relationships

The Observatory depends upon:

Development Systems.

It is the window through which every other specification becomes visible to the player.

# Closing Statement

A traveler stood upon a mountain overlooking a continent.

Below lay forests, rivers, villages, cities, roads, and distant kingdoms.

To the eye, it was only a landscape.

To the Observatory, it was thousands of years of interconnected history.

Every river had redirected migrations.

Every road carried forgotten merchants.

Every monument honored lives long gone.

Every forest remembered the hands that planted it.

The world had always been telling its story.

The Observatory simply taught the traveler how to see it.
