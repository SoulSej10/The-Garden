# Purpose

This document defines the intended emotional and cognitive experience of interacting with The Garden.

Every system, interface, visualization, and interaction should contribute toward a consistent player experience centered on curiosity, understanding, reflection, and discovery.

The goal is not to maximize engagement through constant stimulation.

The goal is to cultivate lasting fascination with a living world.

---

# Core Principle

**The player should feel like an observer within a living world, not an operator managing a simulation.**

The Garden is experienced through discovery rather than direction.

The world invites attention.

It never demands it.

---

# Design Philosophy

The player experience should encourage:

Curiosity.

Wonder.

Patience.

Reflection.

Learning.

Empathy.

Exploration.

Discovery.

The Observatory should create moments where understanding itself becomes rewarding.

---

# Emotional Experience

Players should naturally experience:

Wonder when discovering something unexpected.

Satisfaction when understanding complex systems.

Attachment to people and places.

Curiosity about unexplained events.

Melancholy when civilizations disappear.

Hope when ecosystems recover.

Pride when long-term efforts bear fruit.

Reflection when history reveals hidden connections.

These emotions emerge from observation rather than scripted storytelling.

---

# Cognitive Experience

The Observatory should help players answer progressively deeper questions.

Examples include:

What happened?

↓

Why did it happen?

↓

What caused it?

↓

What changed because of it?

↓

What might happen next?

Each answer should reveal another layer of understanding.

---

# Pace of Interaction

The experience should feel:

Calm.

Intentional.

Readable.

Comfortable.

Players should never feel pressured to react immediately.

The world continues naturally regardless of the player's pace.

---

# Player Agency

Players remain free to:

Observe.

Intervene.

Experiment.

Document.

Ignore.

Return later.

Every style of play is equally valid.

The Observatory supports choice without prescribing it.

---

# Information Flow

Information should appear in layers.

Initial observations remain simple.

Additional context becomes available through exploration.

Advanced analysis is always optional.

The player decides how deeply to investigate.

---

# Respect for Attention

The Garden values the player's attention.

The Observatory avoids:

Notification fatigue.

Excessive alerts.

Persistent interruptions.

Artificial urgency.

Interface clutter.

Every presented detail should earn the player's attention.

---

# Long-Term Engagement

The Observatory encourages continued interest through:

Changing worlds.

Evolving civilizations.

Unexpected discoveries.

Historical continuity.

Scientific progress.

Environmental transformation.

Returning players should find genuinely new stories waiting for them.

---

# Accessibility of Knowledge

Understanding should never require expertise.

Simple observations should remain intuitive.

Complex systems should become understandable through progressive exploration.

The Observatory teaches naturally through interaction.

---

# State Variables

The Observatory observes:

Player Curiosity.

Discovery Progress.

Observation History.

Preferred Investigation Depth.

Interaction Patterns.

Knowledge Growth.

These values personalize presentation without changing the underlying simulation.

Every Observatory feature contributes to one coherent experience.

# Closing Statement

A player watched a river for several minutes.

Nothing extraordinary happened.

Fish moved with the current.

Farmers gathered water.

Children played nearby.

Years later, the player returned.

The river had shifted.

Fields had expanded.

A bridge connected two growing towns.

Trade flourished.

What first appeared ordinary slowly revealed centuries of change.

The player had not been entertained.

They had learned to notice.

In The Garden, the greatest discoveries belong to those who take the time to observe.
