# Purpose

This document defines how worlds are preserved, revisited, compared, and replayed within The Garden.

Saving is not merely recording player progress.

It is preserving the complete state of a living universe.

Replay is not about repeating gameplay.

It is about revisiting history.

---

# Core Principle

**Every save is a historical snapshot of an evolving world.**

A saved world captures a unique moment in time.

Loading restores that moment.

Replay explores how that moment came to be and what followed from it.

---

# Design Philosophy

Players should feel like historians returning to an archived civilization.

A save is not only a checkpoint.

It is a preserved chapter in the world's ongoing history.

Every snapshot has historical value.

---

# World Persistence

A save preserves the complete simulation state, including:

Geography.

Climate.

Ecosystems.

Citizens.

Families.

Civilizations.

Infrastructure.

Scientific progress.

Historical records.

World Memory.

The restored world should behave exactly as it did when the snapshot was created.

---

# Save Philosophy

Players may save at meaningful moments.

Examples include:

Beginning of a civilization.

Scientific revolution.

Environmental restoration.

Major migration.

Golden age.

Civilizational collapse.

Personal discoveries.

The value of a save comes from its historical context rather than its gameplay progression.

---

# Replay Philosophy

Replay allows players to observe history unfolding again.

Replay is observational.

It does not overwrite the original historical record.

The player studies the past without changing it.

---

# Historical Playback

Players may replay:

Entire world history.

Civilization history.

Citizen lives.

Family histories.

Wars.

Scientific discoveries.

Environmental change.

Migration routes.

Replay should preserve historical accuracy while allowing flexible observation.

---

# Temporal Controls

Replay supports:

Pause.

Resume.

Slow motion.

Accelerated time.

Frame-by-frame progression.

Jump to significant events.

Continuous timeline navigation.

Time controls exist to improve understanding rather than create spectacle.

---

# Comparative Replay

Players may compare:

Two historical periods.

Different civilizations.

Alternative save points.

Environmental recovery.

Population growth.

Political evolution.

Comparisons reveal change across time without altering recorded history.

---

# Save Management

The Observatory should organize worlds through:

Chronology.

Historical significance.

Player annotations.

Bookmarks.

Civilization milestones.

Custom labels.

Searching for a save should feel like exploring an archive rather than managing files.

---

# Historical Integrity

Replays are immutable historical records.

Observation never changes history.

If players wish to explore an alternate future, they create a new timeline by loading an earlier save and allowing the simulation to continue from that point.

Each resulting timeline becomes its own valid historical branch.

---

# Timeline Branching

Loading an earlier save creates the possibility of divergent histories.

For example:

Year 1200

↓

Save

↓

Timeline A

↓

Year 5000

or

↓

Reload Year 1200

↓

Different interventions

↓

Timeline B

↓

Year 5000

Each branch represents a unique historical evolution.

No branch is considered the "correct" one.

# Closing Statement

A player saved their world during the founding of a small riverside village.

Thousands of simulated years later, that village had become the heart of a vast civilization.

Curiosity led the player back to the original save.

The same river flowed.

The same hills stood nearby.

Only a handful of families lived there.

The player watched history unfold once more.

Not because they wished to change it.

Because they wanted to understand how something so ordinary became something extraordinary.

In The Garden, saving a world is not preserving progress.

It is preserving history itself.
