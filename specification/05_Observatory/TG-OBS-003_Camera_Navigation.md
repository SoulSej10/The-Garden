# Purpose

This document defines how players observe, navigate, and explore the world of The Garden.

The camera is not merely a control mechanism.

It is the player's primary instrument for understanding a living simulation.

Navigation should encourage exploration, observation, and contextual awareness rather than rapid movement between isolated screens.

---

# Core Principle

**The camera should feel like curiosity made visible.**

Every movement should reveal relationships.

Every zoom level should provide meaningful information.

Every transition should preserve the player's understanding of where they are within the world.

---

# Design Philosophy

Navigation should never disconnect the player from the world.

Moving from a continent to a citizen should feel like examining different scales of the same living system.

There should never be a sense of "loading another game."

Only a deeper level of observation.

---

# Observation Scales

The Observatory supports seamless transitions between multiple scales.

Planetary.

Continental.

Regional.

Landscape.

Settlement.

District.

Building.

Individual.

The player should move naturally between these perspectives without losing context.

---

# Zoom Philosophy

Every zoom level should answer different questions.

Planetary

How is the world changing?

Continental

How are civilizations expanding?

Regional

How are ecosystems interacting?

Settlement

How is this community developing?

Building

What is happening here?

Individual

Who is this person?

Zooming should reveal new understanding rather than simply larger graphics.

---

# Navigation Principles

Navigation should be:

Continuous.

Predictable.

Responsive.

Context preserving.

Comfortable.

Exploration focused.

The player should always know:

Where they are.

What they are observing.

How they arrived there.

---

# Following the World

Players may naturally follow:

Citizens.

Families.

Animals.

Rivers.

Roads.

Trade caravans.

Migration routes.

Weather systems.

Historical events.

Following an entity should preserve awareness of its broader context within the world.

---

# Context Preservation

Changing focus should maintain orientation.

Examples include:

Citizen → Home.

Home → Settlement.

Settlement → Region.

Region → Civilization.

Civilization → Continent.

The player should always be able to understand how local observations relate to the larger world.

---

# Navigation History

The Observatory remembers exploration.

Players may revisit:

Previous locations.

Historical events.

Observed citizens.

Important discoveries.

Bookmarked places.

Recently explored regions.

Navigation history supports long-term investigation.

---

# Time-Aware Navigation

Navigation extends beyond space.

Players should be able to move through:

Present.

Recent history.

Ancient history.

Recorded events.

Historical reconstructions.

The player navigates both geography and chronology.

# Closing Statement

A player noticed smoke rising from a distant valley.

They zoomed closer.

A village struggled through a harsh winter.

They followed a road leading away from the settlement.

The road reached another town.

Trade had stopped.

The player traced the route farther.

A bridge had collapsed months earlier during severe flooding.

One broken bridge.

Two isolated settlements.

A failing harvest.

A migration.

The beginning of a new chapter in history.

The player never searched for a disaster.

They simply followed a trail of questions.

In The Garden, every journey begins with looking a little closer.
