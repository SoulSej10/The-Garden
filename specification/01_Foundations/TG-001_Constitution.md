# The Garden Design Bible

# Document 01 — Design Pillars & The Garden Constitution

**Document ID:** TG-001

**Version:** 0.1

**Status:** Living Document

**Priority:** CRITICAL

---

# Purpose

This document establishes the immutable principles that govern every aspect of The Garden.

These are constitutional laws.

Every feature, simulation rule, UI component, AI prompt, architectural decision, and future expansion must comply with these principles.

If a proposed feature violates this constitution, the feature must be redesigned.

The constitution is intentionally difficult to change.

---

# The Garden Constitution

## Law I

# The World Exists Independently

The world does not exist because a player is observing it.

The world exists because the simulation exists.

Players may disconnect.

Applications may close.

The simulation may pause for technical reasons.

Conceptually, however, the world is always moving forward.

The Garden never pretends that time only exists while someone is watching.

---

## Law II

# Nothing Happens Without Cause

Every meaningful event must have a reason.

There shall be no arbitrary events.

Examples:

❌ A village randomly disappears.

✔ The village disappears because:

* disease spread
* famine occurred
* migration happened
* resources were exhausted
* conflict emerged

The simulation should always be explainable.

---

## Law III

# Systems Create Stories

Developers do not write stories.

Developers write systems.

Stories emerge from the interaction of those systems.

The Story Engine never invents facts.

It interprets facts produced by the simulation.

---

## Law IV

# History Is Permanent

History cannot be rewritten.

Every meaningful event becomes part of the world's memory.

Examples:

Births

Deaths

Marriages

Founding villages

Wars

Discoveries

Natural disasters

Leadership changes

History is immutable.

History is one of the simulation's primary products.

---

## Law V

# The Simulation Owns Reality

The UI never decides what is true.

The UI only displays reality.

Example:

Incorrect:

Player presses "Harvest"

↓

Food instantly increases.

Correct:

Player requests harvest.

↓

Simulation evaluates weather.

↓

Simulation evaluates available workers.

↓

Simulation evaluates crop maturity.

↓

Simulation decides outcome.

---

## Law VI

# Every Citizen Is Equal

No citizen receives hidden advantages because they are important.

Kings.

Farmers.

Children.

Merchants.

Everyone follows the same simulation rules.

Importance emerges naturally through history.

---

## Law VII

# Simplicity Before Complexity

Every new feature must begin as a simple rule.

Complexity should emerge from combinations of simple rules.

Avoid giant all-knowing systems.

Prefer many small interacting systems.

---

## Law VIII

# Every System Must Influence Another

No isolated mechanics.

Weather influences crops.

Crops influence food.

Food influences health.

Health influences work.

Work influences economy.

Economy influences politics.

Everything connects.

Disconnected mechanics should not exist.

---

## Law IX

# Time Moves Forward

Time never rewinds.

Undo mechanics should never modify history.

Instead:

History records corrections.

The past remains observable.

---

## Law X

# The Player Is A Gardener

Players influence conditions.

Players rarely dictate outcomes.

The world should retain agency.

Players cultivate.

The world responds.

---

# Design Pillars

## Pillar One

### Emergence

The simulation should surprise both developers and players.

Unexpected outcomes are considered successes.

---

## Pillar Two

### Persistence

The world accumulates history.

Every generation inherits consequences from previous generations.

---

## Pillar Three

### Explainability

Every event should have traceable causes.

The player should be able to answer:

"Why did this happen?"

through observation of the simulation.

---

## Pillar Four

### Believability

Realism is not required.

Believability is.

If players understand why something happened, the simulation succeeds.

---

## Pillar Five

### Transparency

The simulation should expose enough information for players to investigate.

Hidden calculations should be minimized.

Curiosity should be rewarded.

---

# Forbidden Design Patterns

The following are strongly discouraged unless absolutely necessary.

## Random Events Without Context

Storms, wars, migrations, or famines should emerge from environmental conditions whenever possible.

Randomness may influence probability.

Randomness should not replace causality.

---

## Artificial Importance

No citizen should secretly receive "main character" status.

Importance emerges through action.

---

## Scripted Destiny

No predetermined hero.

No predetermined villain.

No required ending.

The simulation remains open-ended.

---

## Invisible Simulation

Players should always have tools to understand what is happening.

Graphs.

Logs.

Stories.

Timelines.

Inspectors.

Explanations.

A living world should also be an observable world.

---

# Decision Framework

Whenever a new feature is proposed, evaluate it against these questions:

1. Does it increase autonomy?
2. Does it strengthen interconnected systems?
3. Can it produce new emergent stories?
4. Can its outcome be explained?
5. Does it preserve history?
6. Does it respect the world's independence?

If the answer to most questions is "no," redesign the feature.

---

# Architectural Motto

> We do not script stories.

> We build worlds capable of creating them.

---

# Closing Statement

The Garden is not built feature by feature.

It is cultivated law by law.

Every future system—from weather to civilization, from a single flower to an entire empire—must remain faithful to these constitutional principles.

The Constitution exists so that The Garden always grows in the same direction, no matter how large it becomes.
