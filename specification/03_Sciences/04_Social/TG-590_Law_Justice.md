# Purpose

Law and Justice define how societies formalize expectations, resolve disputes, protect rights, assign responsibilities, and maintain long-term social stability.

Law transforms informal social norms into structured institutions capable of governing large and complex civilizations.

Justice seeks legitimacy by balancing fairness, order, accountability, and social trust.

The Garden models legal systems as evolving institutions rather than static rulebooks.

---

# Scientific Basis

Legal systems emerge as populations grow beyond the scale where personal relationships and reputation alone can resolve disputes.

Initially, communities rely upon customs and shared norms.

As societies become larger and more specialized, these customs become formalized into legal principles, institutions, procedures, and professional roles.

Justice continuously evolves alongside society.

# Closing Statement

Long before judges wore robes, two neighbors argued over the boundary between their fields.

The village elder listened.

The community agreed upon a fair solution.

Years later, another dispute arose.

The elder remembered the earlier decision.

Eventually the decisions were written.

Generations passed.

The elder's name disappeared.

The principles remained.

In The Garden, justice is not born from written laws.

Written laws are born from generations seeking justice.
