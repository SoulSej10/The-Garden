# Purpose

Decision Making defines how intelligent agents choose actions.

Every meaningful behavior within The Garden originates from the evaluation of perception, memory, emotion, motivation, knowledge, relationships, personality, and environmental conditions.

This document establishes a unified decision architecture shared by every intelligent individual in the simulation.

It is the behavioral heart of The Garden.

---

# Scientific Basis

Real-world decision making is bounded, probabilistic, emotional, and context dependent.

Individuals rarely calculate perfect solutions.

Instead, they continuously evaluate competing possibilities using incomplete information and personal priorities.

The Garden models decision making as an adaptive cognitive process rather than deterministic scripting.

---

# Design Philosophy

No action exists without cause.

Every decision has history.

Every choice reflects a lifetime of experiences.

Every consequence becomes part of future decisions.

The simulation never asks:

> "What should happen?"

It asks:

> "Given who this individual has become, what would they most likely choose?"

---

# The Decision Cycle

Every cognitive decision follows a continuous evaluation loop.

```text
Perception
      ↓
Memory Recall
      ↓
Emotional Evaluation
      ↓
Motivational Analysis
      ↓
Generate Possible Actions
      ↓
Predict Outcomes
      ↓
Evaluate Consequences
      ↓
Select Action
      ↓
Execute Action
      ↓
Observe Results
      ↓
Learn
      ↓
Repeat
```

This loop operates continuously throughout an agent's lifetime.

# Utility

The Garden avoids rigid mathematical optimization.

Instead, every action produces a contextual utility estimate.

Utility represents:

Expected benefit.

Expected cost.

Uncertainty.

Personal value.

Future opportunity.

Different individuals assign different utility to identical actions.

---

# Risk Assessment

Agents evaluate uncertainty before acting.

Risk depends upon:

Known danger.

Unknown danger.

Past experience.

Confidence.

Personality.

Current emotional state.

Available alternatives.

Fearful individuals tolerate less uncertainty.

Curious individuals often tolerate more.

---

# Opportunity Cost

Choosing one action means abandoning others.

Decision making therefore includes:

Immediate sacrifice.

Future opportunity.

Resource commitment.

Time investment.

Relationship consequences.

Every choice closes other possibilities.

Immediate survival.

# Habit

Frequently repeated decisions gradually become habits.

Habits:

Reduce cognitive effort.

Increase behavioral consistency.

May persist even when no longer optimal.

Habits may strengthen or weaken over time.

# Performance Considerations

Decision evaluation should prioritize only behaviorally relevant actions.

Large action spaces should be pruned using context before evaluation.

Long-term planning should be event-driven rather than continuously recalculated.

Behavior trees, utility systems, planners, or future AI implementations may be used internally provided they preserve the behavioral principles defined by this specification.
