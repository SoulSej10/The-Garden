# Purpose

Perception defines how intelligent agents observe, interpret, and become aware of the world around them.

Perception is not reality.

It is the agent's current understanding of reality based on limited sensory information, attention, previous experience, and environmental conditions.

Every future cognitive process begins with perception.

No intelligent decision can occur without first perceiving the world.

---

# Scientific Basis

Perception is the process of transforming environmental stimuli into meaningful internal representations.

It is selective, incomplete, and continuously changing.

Every organism perceives only a fraction of its environment.

The Garden models perception as a dynamic process constrained by biology, environment, attention, and uncertainty.

---

# Design Philosophy

The world exists independently.

Perception does not reveal all of it.

Every intelligent being lives inside a personal version of reality.

No two agents perceive the world in exactly the same way.

Knowledge begins where perception succeeds.

Ignorance begins where it fails.

# Relationship to Civilization

Civilizations never possess complete knowledge.

Explorers discover unknown lands because nobody has seen them before.

Generals misjudge battlefields because information arrives too late.

Merchants overlook opportunities hidden beyond familiar roads.

Leaders act upon what they believe the world to be—not always what it truly is.

Every great historical success and every catastrophic mistake begins with perception.

# Closing Statement

The mountain was always there.

The traveler simply had not seen it.

The storm was already approaching.

The fisherman noticed only calm water.

The wolf watched quietly from the trees.

The deer never looked in the right direction.

The world reveals itself only in fragments.

Every living mind must assemble those fragments into a picture of reality.

Sometimes that picture is accurate.

Sometimes it changes history.

In The Garden, perception is not seeing everything.

It is living with the uncertainty of never seeing enough.
