# Purpose

Extinction & Recovery defines how species disappear, ecosystems collapse, and life rebuilds itself over time.

Extinction is a natural outcome of changing environments, ecological competition, catastrophic events, and evolutionary history.

Recovery is the ability of ecosystems to reorganize, regenerate, and establish new forms of ecological balance.

Together, these processes ensure that The Garden remains dynamic across centuries and millennia.

---

# Scientific Basis

Species continuously face extinction risk.

Environmental change, ecological pressure, and random events influence long-term survival.

Following disturbance, ecosystems rarely return to their previous state.

Instead, ecological succession and adaptation create new communities.

The Garden models extinction and recovery as continuous ecological processes rather than isolated events.

---

# Design Philosophy

Nothing living is guaranteed permanence.

Every species exists only while the world continues to support it.

When life disappears, the world does not end.

It changes.

Recovery does not erase loss.

It builds upon it.

Nature remembers every extinction through the ecosystems that follow.

# Extinction

Extinction occurs when a species can no longer maintain viable populations.

Possible causes include:

Climate change.

Habitat loss.

Predation.

Disease.

Competition.

Food scarcity.

Environmental disasters.

Human activity.

Most extinctions should be preceded by observable ecological decline.

---

# Local Extinction

Species may disappear from individual regions while surviving elsewhere.

Local extinction influences:

Food webs.

Migration.

Competition.

Biodiversity.

Civilization.

Regional loss is often reversible through recolonization.

---

# Global Extinction

Global extinction occurs when no surviving populations remain.

Extinct species no longer participate in ecological systems.

Historical records preserve their existence.

Their ecological roles become vacant.

---

# Ecological Cascades

Species loss may trigger secondary consequences.

Examples include:

Predator decline.

Prey population explosions.

Habitat transformation.

Pollination failure.

Forest restructuring.

Food web collapse.

The loss of one species may reshape entire ecosystems.

---

# Recovery

Recovery occurs through:

Habitat regeneration.

Population growth.

Migration.

Ecological succession.

Reduced environmental pressure.

Recovery rarely restores previous conditions exactly.

New ecological equilibria emerge.

---

# Rewilding

Abandoned landscapes naturally recover.

Examples include:

Former farmland becoming forest.

Abandoned villages returning to wilderness.

River systems reclaiming engineered channels.

Grasslands replacing degraded terrain.

Nature actively rebuilds where pressure declines.

---

# Ecological Memory

Ecosystems retain historical influence.

Previous disturbances continue affecting:

Species composition.

Soil quality.

Hydrology.

Forest age.

Habitat complexity.

History remains visible within landscapes.
