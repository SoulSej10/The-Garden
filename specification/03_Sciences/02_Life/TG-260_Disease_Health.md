# Purpose

Disease & Health defines how organisms maintain biological well-being, respond to illness, recover from injury, and interact with infectious agents.

Health is not merely the absence of disease.

It is the ability of an organism to survive, recover, reproduce, and continue participating in the living world.

Disease is a natural ecological process that influences populations, ecosystems, and eventually civilizations.

---

# Scientific Basis

Health emerges from interactions between organisms, their environment, nutrition, stress, genetics (future), and infectious agents.

Disease spreads through ecological relationships rather than arbitrary game mechanics.

The Garden models health as a dynamic biological state influenced by multiple interacting systems.

---

# Design Philosophy

Life is resilient.

Illness is inevitable.

Recovery is possible.

Health is constantly earned rather than permanently possessed.

Disease is neither punishment nor randomness.

It is one of nature's methods of maintaining ecological balance.

---

# Core Concepts

Every living organism possesses health.

Health changes continuously according to:

Nutrition.

Hydration.

Rest.

Stress.

Environmental conditions.

Injury.

Disease exposure.

Age.

No organism remains perfectly healthy forever.

---

# Health

Health represents overall biological condition.

Healthy organisms:

Grow efficiently.

Reproduce successfully.

Recover quickly.

Resist disease.

Maintain higher survival probabilities.

Health continuously fluctuates.

---

# Disease

Disease represents biological dysfunction caused by:

Pathogens.

Parasites.

Environmental stress.

Nutritional deficiency.

Toxins (future).

Genetic disorders (future).

Disease varies in severity, duration, and transmission.

---

# Pathogens

Pathogens include:

Bacteria.

Viruses.

Fungi.

Parasites.

Unknown future pathogens.

The simulation models pathogen categories rather than individual species unless historically significant.

---

# Transmission

Disease may spread through:

Direct contact.

Airborne exposure.

Water.

Food.

Vectors.

Waste.

Population density.

Transmission probability depends upon environmental and biological conditions.

---

# Immunity

Organisms possess varying resistance to disease.

Immunity may arise through:

Natural recovery.

Species adaptation.

Environmental health.

Future biological systems.

Immunity reduces—but rarely eliminates—the risk of infection.

---

# Injury

Health declines through injury as well as disease.

Common causes include:

Predation.

Accidents.

Conflict.

Environmental hazards.

Natural disasters.

Injuries may heal over time depending upon health and environmental conditions.

---

# Recovery

Recovery depends upon:

Nutrition.

Hydration.

Rest.

Environmental safety.

Medical intervention (future civilizations).

Species resilience.

Poor conditions delay or prevent recovery.

---

# Epidemics

Large outbreaks may emerge naturally.

Contributing factors include:

High population density.

Poor sanitation.

Migration.

Climate conditions.

Food shortages.

Novel pathogens.

Epidemics should emerge rather than be scripted.

Population density increases epidemic risk.

Disease never exists without biological causes.

# Performance Considerations

Disease calculations should prioritize regional and population-level dynamics.

Persistent individual simulation is reserved for historically significant organisms or nearby entities.

Statistical transmission models are preferred over exhaustive pairwise simulation.

# Relationship to Civilization

Disease has shaped civilizations as profoundly as war.

Entire cities have declined through epidemics.

Trade routes have spread pathogens.

Medical knowledge has extended lifespans.

Public sanitation has transformed societies.

Every civilization must eventually confront disease—not as an enemy to eliminate, but as a permanent part of the living world.

Health becomes one of civilization's greatest responsibilities.

# Closing Statement

Every living thing carries invisible companions.

Some nourish.

Some protect.

Some bring illness.

A wounded deer may recover beside a quiet stream.

A crowded colony may unknowingly begin an epidemic.

A single infection may change the course of generations.

Disease is not separate from life.

It travels beside it.

And health is not a permanent gift.

It is a fragile balance, renewed every day by the countless choices made by living organisms and the world that surrounds them.

In The Garden, even the unseen has the power to shape history.
