# Purpose

Biology Foundations defines the universal principles shared by every living organism in The Garden.

Rather than describing specific species, this document establishes the common biological rules that govern life itself.

Every plant, fungus, microorganism, animal, and intelligent species inherits these principles.

This specification provides a consistent biological framework for the entire simulation.

---

# Scientific Basis

Life is defined by a collection of shared characteristics.

Living organisms:

Consume energy.

Grow.

Respond to their environment.

Maintain internal stability.

Reproduce.

Age.

Die.

The Garden models these behaviors in a simplified but internally consistent manner to support emergent ecosystems and civilizations.

---

# Design Philosophy

Life is not a collection of statistics.

Life is a process.

Every organism is continuously changing.

Nothing living remains perfectly static.

Birth begins a journey.

Death completes it.

The simulation should portray life as a dynamic cycle rather than a permanent object.

---

# Core Characteristics of Life

Every living organism possesses:

Identity

A unique existence within the simulation.

Energy

A reserve used to sustain biological processes.

Growth

Development through distinct life stages.

Health

The current biological condition of the organism.

Needs

Requirements necessary for continued survival.

Adaptation

The ability to respond to changing environmental conditions.

Reproduction

The capacity to produce future generations.

Mortality

The certainty that every organism eventually dies.

---

# Universal Life Cycle

All organisms follow a generalized biological cycle.

```text
Birth
    ↓
Juvenile Development
    ↓
Maturity
    ↓
Reproduction
    ↓
Aging
    ↓
Death
```

Different organisms may spend vastly different amounts of time within each stage.

The structure, however, remains universal.

---

# Energy

Life requires energy.

Energy is obtained through different biological strategies depending on the organism.

Examples include:

Photosynthesis.

Consumption.

Decomposition.

Symbiosis (future).

Without sufficient energy:

Growth slows.

Health declines.

Reproduction decreases.

Death eventually occurs.

---

# Growth

Organisms develop over time.

Growth influences:

Size.

Capability.

Reproductive maturity.

Survival probability.

Growth rates vary by species and environmental conditions.

---

# Homeostasis

Living organisms attempt to maintain internal stability despite environmental change.

Examples include:

Temperature regulation.

Hydration.

Nutrition.

Stress response.

Failure to maintain homeostasis gradually reduces health.

---

# Adaptation

Living organisms respond to environmental conditions.

Adaptation may occur through:

Behavior.

Physiology.

Evolution (future).

Migration.

Adaptation improves survival without requiring perfect optimization.

---

# Mortality

Every organism eventually dies.

Death may result from:

Old age.

Disease.

Predation.

Starvation.

Environmental hazards.

Accidents.

Death is a natural biological process and an essential component of ecological renewal.

---

# Reproduction

Organisms produce future generations.

Reproductive success depends upon:

Health.

Environmental suitability.

Resource availability.

Population dynamics.

Species-specific behavior.

Successful reproduction ensures long-term population persistence.

# Simulation Rules

All living organisms follow universal biological principles.

Rules include:

Energy is continuously consumed.

Growth requires energy.

Poor environmental conditions increase stress.

Stress reduces biological performance.

Healthy organisms reproduce more successfully.

Death removes organisms from active biological processes while contributing to ecological cycles.

# Edge Cases

The framework should support:

Extremely short-lived organisms.

Long-lived organisms.

Dormant life stages.

Seasonal reproduction.

Environmental dormancy.

Slow biological growth.

Rapid population turnover.

Species-specific exceptions should extend rather than replace the universal framework.

Species-specific behaviors should extend this framework without duplicating core biological logic.

# Relationship to Civilization

Civilizations are biological systems before they become social systems.

Every citizen is first an organism.

They must eat.

Rest.

Grow.

Age.

Become ill.

Recover.

Eventually die.

Society emerges from biology, not the other way around.

The laws of life apply equally to the smallest insect and the greatest emperor.
