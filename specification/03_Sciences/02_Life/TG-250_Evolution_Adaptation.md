# Purpose

Evolution & Adaptation defines how life gradually changes across generations in response to environmental pressures.

Evolution is not progress.

It is not optimization.

It is not destiny.

It is the accumulation of small changes that improve survival within a particular environment.

This document establishes the long-term adaptability of life throughout The Garden.

---

# Scientific Basis

Evolution occurs when heritable variation interacts with natural selection.

Individuals do not evolve.

Populations evolve over many generations.

Environmental conditions determine which traits become more common over time.

The Garden models these processes through simplified adaptive systems while preserving their emergent nature.

---

# Design Philosophy

Life does not seek perfection.

It seeks continuation.

Every adaptation represents a compromise.

A trait beneficial in one environment may become harmful in another.

There is no final form.

Only changing circumstances.

# Core Concepts

Evolution operates through:

Variation.

Inheritance.

Selection.

Time.

Chance.

Environmental pressure.

Adaptation emerges gradually rather than instantly.

---

# Adaptation

Adaptation represents improved fitness within a specific environment.

Examples include:

Cold tolerance.

Heat tolerance.

Drought resistance.

Disease resistance.

Behavioral flexibility.

Migration strategies.

Camouflage.

Adaptations are always contextual.

No adaptation is universally superior.

---

# Selective Pressure

Selective pressures influence reproductive success.

Common pressures include:

Climate.

Food availability.

Predation.

Disease.

Competition.

Habitat change.

Natural disasters.

Human activity (future).

Selective pressures continually reshape populations.

---

# Variation

Individuals possess natural variation.

Variation influences:

Survival.

Growth.

Reproduction.

Behavior.

Environmental tolerance.

Variation provides the raw material for evolution.

---

# Inheritance

Successful traits may be inherited by future generations.

Inheritance is represented abstractly rather than through complete genetic simulation.

The system focuses on long-term population trends rather than molecular biology.

---

# Evolutionary Time

Evolution operates across long timescales.

Meaningful adaptation generally requires:

Multiple generations.

Persistent environmental conditions.

Population continuity.

Rapid environmental change may outpace evolutionary adaptation.

---

# Behavioral Adaptation

Not every adaptation is physical.

Species may gradually develop:

Migration timing.

Foraging strategies.

Predator avoidance.

Social organization.

Breeding behavior.

Behavior evolves alongside biology.

---

# Environmental Mismatch

An adaptation that succeeds today may fail tomorrow.

Examples include:

Climate shifts.

Habitat loss.

New predators.

Emerging diseases.

Changing food sources.

Adaptation never guarantees permanent success.

---

# Speciation (Future)

Long-term isolation may eventually produce new species.

Speciation requires:

Geographic separation.

Reproductive isolation.

Extended evolutionary time.

Persistent environmental differences.

Initial releases of The Garden do not require full speciation mechanics.

---

# Extinction Pressure

Failure to adapt increases extinction risk.

Extinction results from prolonged inability to survive changing conditions.

Evolution reduces—but never eliminates—this risk.

# Simulation Rules

Evolution is gradual.

Rules include:

Environmental pressures influence reproductive success.

Adaptive traits become more common over generations.

Poorly adapted populations decline.

Stable environments slow evolutionary change.

Rapid environmental shifts increase selection pressure.

Evolution has no predetermined direction.
