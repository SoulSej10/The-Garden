# Purpose

Domestication & Coevolution defines how intelligent species gradually form long-term biological relationships with other living organisms.

Domestication is not ownership.

It is a process of mutual adaptation.

Over generations, humans change plants and animals through selective pressures while those same organisms reshape human civilization, culture, economy, and survival.

This document establishes the biological foundation for agriculture, livestock, companionship, transportation, and future civilization.

---

# Scientific Basis

Domestication occurs when a species repeatedly benefits from close association with another species.

Artificial selection gradually alters populations across generations.

Coevolution describes reciprocal evolutionary change between interacting species.

The Garden models these processes as long-term adaptive relationships emerging from repeated interaction rather than instantaneous transformation.

---

# Design Philosophy

No species was created to serve another.

Relationships develop because both participants gain advantages.

Some partnerships become temporary.

Others endure for thousands of years.

Civilization is built not only by humans, but by every species that chooses to grow alongside them.

# Artificial Selection

Intelligent species influence future generations by favoring desirable traits.

Selection may favor:

Yield.

Temperament.

Growth rate.

Strength.

Disease resistance.

Productivity.

Appearance.

Artificial selection accelerates biological change without replacing natural evolution.

---

# Coevolution

Both species influence one another.

Examples include:

Dogs becoming companions while humans improve hunting success.

Crop cultivation encouraging permanent settlements.

Livestock changing dietary habits.

Pollinators adapting to cultivated plants.

Relationships evolve in both directions.

---

# Taming vs Domestication

The Garden distinguishes between:

Taming

A behavioral relationship affecting an individual organism.

Domestication

A biological relationship affecting entire populations across generations.

A tame wolf is not a domesticated species.

---

# Dependency

Domesticated organisms may gradually become dependent upon civilization.

Likewise, civilizations become dependent upon domesticated species.

Mutual dependency strengthens long-term relationships but may increase vulnerability if either partner disappears.
