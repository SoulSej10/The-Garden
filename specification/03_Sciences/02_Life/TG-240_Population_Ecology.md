# Purpose

Population Ecology defines how individual organisms collectively become populations, communities, and ecological systems.

Its purpose is to transform countless local decisions into large-scale environmental patterns.

No population is directly controlled.

Population-level behavior emerges from the interactions of individuals with one another and with their environment.

---

# Scientific Basis

Population ecology studies how populations change over time through births, deaths, migration, competition, predation, resource availability, and environmental conditions.

The Garden models these principles as emergent outcomes rather than predetermined trajectories.

Large-scale ecological behavior is an emergent consequence of many independent biological processes.

# Design Philosophy

History belongs to populations.

# Carrying Capacity

Every ecosystem possesses a carrying capacity.

This represents the sustainable number of organisms that available resources can support.

Carrying capacity varies according to:

Biome

Climate

Water

Food

Habitat

Season

Environmental health

Population pressure naturally increases as carrying capacity is approached.

---

# Predator–Prey Dynamics

Predators and prey influence one another continuously.

Examples:

Increasing prey populations support predator growth.

Growing predator populations reduce prey abundance.

Declining prey eventually reduce predator survival.

These oscillations should emerge naturally without artificial balancing.

# Relationship to Civilization

Long before villages existed, populations already shaped the world.

Migrating herds transformed grasslands.

Predators regulated prey.

Beavers redirected rivers.

Pollinators sustained forests.

Human civilization inherits these ecological processes rather than replacing them.

The same principles governing animal populations will later govern towns, cities, kingdoms, and civilizations.

Emergence does not begin with humanity.

It begins with life itself.
