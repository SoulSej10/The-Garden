# Purpose

Natural Resources define the materials and environmental assets that sustain life, enable civilization, and drive economic development.

Resources are not independent objects.

They emerge from the interaction of geological, hydrological, climatic, and biological systems.

Every resource has an origin.

Every resource has limits.

Every resource has consequences.

---

# Scientific Basis

Natural resources are distributed according to environmental conditions rather than arbitrary placement.

Their abundance changes over time through natural regeneration, environmental degradation, and human activity.

The simulation emphasizes sustainability, scarcity, and environmental feedback instead of static inventories.

---

# Design Philosophy

Resources belong to nature first.

Civilizations merely borrow them.

A forest does not exist to provide timber.

It exists as an ecosystem.

Timber becomes available because forests exist.

Likewise, rivers provide fish because healthy aquatic ecosystems support them.

Resources should always feel like part of the living world—not rewards placed for the player.

# Resource Regeneration

Renewable resources regenerate naturally.

Examples:

Forests regrow.

Fish populations recover.

Wildlife reproduces.

Groundwater replenishes.

Crop fertility improves through ecological recovery.

Regeneration depends upon environmental health.

---

# Resource Depletion

Resources decline through excessive extraction.

Possible causes include:

Overharvesting

Mining

Deforestation

Overfishing

Water overuse

Soil exhaustion

Pollution

Climate shifts

Depletion creates long-term consequences.

Recovery often requires decades.

---

# Resource Quality

Not all resources possess equal quality.

Examples:

Old-growth forests produce superior timber.

Rich iron deposits produce higher-quality ore.

Healthy rivers contain abundant fish.

Fertile soils support greater yields.

Quality influences economic value and extraction efficiency.

---

# Sustainability

Every resource system possesses sustainable limits.

Extraction below regeneration supports long-term stability.

Extraction beyond regeneration creates environmental degradation.

Sustainability should emerge naturally rather than through arbitrary restrictions.

# Relationship to Civilization

Civilizations are defined by how they use resources.

Some preserve forests.

Others consume them.

Some invest in irrigation.

Others exhaust rivers.

Some adapt to scarcity.

Others collapse beneath it.

Resources shape trade.

Trade shapes wealth.

Wealth shapes power.

Power shapes history.

The fate of every civilization is inseparable from the health of the natural systems that sustain it.

# Closing Statement

The mountains offer stone.

The forests offer timber.

The rivers offer water.

The fields offer harvests.

Nature gives generously, but never without limits.

Every tree cut today changes tomorrow's forest.

Every mine emptied leaves a silent mountain.

Every healthy ecosystem becomes an inheritance for future generations.

Natural resources are not possessions.

They are promises made by the world—and every civilization must decide whether to honor or break those promises.
