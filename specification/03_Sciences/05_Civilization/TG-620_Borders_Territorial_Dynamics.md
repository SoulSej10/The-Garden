# Purpose

Borders and Territorial Dynamics define how political boundaries emerge, expand, contract, stabilize, fragment, and disappear over time.

Territory is not static.

It is the continuously changing result of settlement, administration, economics, geography, culture, diplomacy, conflict, and migration.

The Garden models borders as emergent spatial relationships rather than permanently drawn map lines.

---

# Scientific Basis

Historical borders rarely remained fixed.

They shifted because of:

Population movement.

Trade.

Natural barriers.

Wars.

Political agreements.

Economic influence.

Environmental change.

Administrative reform.

Borders therefore evolve as living systems.

---

# Design Philosophy

People settle land long before anyone claims it.

A border is simply civilization's attempt to describe where its influence currently reaches.

Influence changes.

Therefore borders change.

Deserts.

Coastlines.

# Expansion

Territory may expand through:

Settlement.

Migration.

Trade.

Infrastructure.

Diplomacy.

Cultural assimilation.

Military conquest.

Administrative integration.

# Contraction

Territories may shrink because of:

Economic decline.

Population loss.

Environmental disaster.

Political instability.

War.

Secession.

Administrative failure.

Contraction creates opportunities for neighboring civilizations.

---

# Border Stability

Stable borders emerge when:

Administrative control is effective.

Population identifies with the state.

Infrastructure connects regions.

Economic integration is strong.

Neighbors recognize territorial claims.

Instability increases when these conditions weaken.

Military outposts.

Migration corridors.

Hybrid identities.

Borderlands may persist for centuries.

# Performance Considerations

Territorial influence should be calculated through regional influence fields rather than per-tile ownership whenever possible.

Border recalculation should occur only after meaningful demographic, political, economic, or military changes.

Historical territorial transitions should preserve causality without recording every minor fluctuation.

# Closing Statement

A shepherd crossed the same valley every spring.

No one questioned it.

Years later, farmers settled there.

A road was built.

A market appeared.

Officials arrived to collect taxes.

Soon another kingdom claimed the opposite hillside.

The valley that once belonged to everyone now belonged to history.

In The Garden, borders are not drawn with ink.

They are written by generations of movement, memory, and human choice.
