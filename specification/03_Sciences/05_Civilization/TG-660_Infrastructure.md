# Purpose

Infrastructure defines the physical and organizational systems that enable civilizations to function efficiently across space and time.

Infrastructure connects settlements, distributes resources, supports institutions, and increases the capacity of civilizations to coordinate complex activities.

The Garden models infrastructure as a dynamic network whose value lies in connectivity rather than individual structures.

---

# Scientific Basis

Throughout history, infrastructure has amplified the productive capacity of societies.

Roads reduced travel time.

Bridges connected regions.

Canals expanded trade.

Aqueducts improved public health.

Ports linked continents.

Communication systems accelerated decision-making.

Infrastructure compounds the effectiveness of nearly every other civilizational system.

# Performance Considerations

Infrastructure should be modeled as connected regional networks rather than isolated structures whenever possible.

Network analysis should prioritize major transportation corridors and strategic links instead of simulating every individual path.

Maintenance calculations should occur periodically rather than continuously.

# Closing Statement

A farmer cleared a narrow path through the forest to reach the nearest village.

Others followed.

The path became a road.

The road attracted traders.

The traders attracted craftsmen.

The craftsmen built homes.

Generations later, historians called it the Great Northern Highway.

Few remembered the farmer.

Yet every traveler unknowingly walked the path they first cleared.

In The Garden, civilizations are remembered by their monuments.

But they are sustained by the roads between them.
