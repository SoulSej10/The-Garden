# Purpose

Trade Networks define how goods, resources, knowledge, culture, capital, and people move between settlements and civilizations.

Trade is not simply an exchange of products.

It is the mechanism through which civilizations become interconnected and mutually influential.

The Garden models trade as an adaptive network that continuously evolves in response to geography, infrastructure, politics, technology, and human behavior.

---

# Scientific Basis

Trade has shaped civilization since the earliest settlements.

Communities exchange resources because no region possesses every necessity or opportunity.

As transportation improves, trade expands beyond local markets into regional and international networks.

Trade simultaneously increases prosperity, interdependence, innovation, and vulnerability.

# Performance Considerations

Trade should be modeled as regional flow networks rather than tracking every individual transaction.

Routine commerce should aggregate into measurable economic indicators while preserving historically significant commercial shifts.

Network optimization should prioritize strategic trade corridors over local exchanges.

# Closing Statement

A merchant loaded salt onto a wagon.

They crossed a mountain pass to trade for grain.

The next year they carried books.

Years later they returned with unfamiliar seeds.

Centuries passed.

The mountain pass became one of the busiest roads in the world.

Empires rose to protect it.

Wars were fought over it.

Yet it all began with someone wondering what might exist beyond the next ridge.

In The Garden, trade is never only the movement of goods.

It is the movement of possibility.
