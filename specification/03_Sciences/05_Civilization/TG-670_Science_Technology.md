# Purpose

Science and Technology define how civilizations observe the world, accumulate knowledge, solve problems, develop innovations, and transform their environment.

Scientific progress is not linear.

It emerges from curiosity, necessity, experimentation, collaboration, education, and accumulated historical knowledge.

The Garden models discovery as an emergent process rather than a predefined technology tree.

---

# Scientific Basis

Throughout history, innovation has rarely followed predetermined paths.

Scientific discoveries arise through observation, experimentation, communication, failure, refinement, and unexpected insight.

Technology spreads through education, trade, migration, diplomacy, and cultural exchange.

Different environments naturally encourage different innovations.

---

# Design Philosophy

Civilizations do not invent technology because a timer finishes.

They invent because reality presents problems worth solving.

Every discovery begins as a question.

# Performance Considerations

Scientific progress should be simulated through knowledge domains and research networks rather than tracking every individual invention.

Routine incremental improvements should aggregate into long-term innovation trends while preserving historically significant breakthroughs.

Technology diffusion should prioritize institutional and trade networks over individual interactions.

# Closing Statement

A farmer wondered why one field produced more grain than another.

A scholar wrote the observation.

An apprentice tested it.

An engineer improved the tools.

A teacher shared the results.

Centuries later, millions harvested food from knowledge that began with a single question.

No one remembered the farmer's name.

The question outlived them.

In The Garden, discoveries are not unlocked.

They are inherited, challenged, improved, and passed to those who continue asking why.
