# Purpose

Warfare defines how civilizations organize, conduct, sustain, and conclude organized conflict.

War is not an isolated system.

It is the culmination of interacting political, economic, social, geographic, technological, and historical pressures.

The Garden models warfare as an emergent consequence of civilization rather than an inevitable activity.

---

# Scientific Basis

Throughout history, organized conflict has emerged when competing interests could no longer be resolved through diplomacy, negotiation, or compromise.

Military institutions evolved to provide defense, deterrence, territorial protection, and, at times, expansion.

The causes and consequences of war are deeply interconnected with every major societal system.

---

# Design Philosophy

Wars do not begin when armies move.

They begin when trust fails.

The battlefield is simply where years of unresolved history become visible.

# Performance Considerations

Routine troop movement should be aggregated whenever possible.

Only strategically significant campaigns, battles, reforms, and peace agreements require long-term historical persistence.

Military simulation should emphasize logistics, command, and strategic objectives over individual combat calculations.

# Closing Statement

A dispute over a single bridge lasted for decades.

Merchants argued.

Officials negotiated.

Soldiers gathered.

Eventually the bridge burned.

Thousands crossed rivers carrying weapons instead of goods.

Years later, a child asked why two neighboring nations spoke different languages despite sharing the same valley.

No one pointed toward the battlefield.

They pointed toward the old bridge.

That was where the war truly began.

In The Garden, battles are remembered.

But the causes that created them are never forgotten.
