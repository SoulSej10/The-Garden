# Purpose

Diplomacy defines how independent political entities communicate, negotiate, cooperate, compete, and influence one another without relying solely on force.

Diplomatic relationships emerge from accumulated historical interactions rather than static relationship values.

Every treaty, trade agreement, cultural exchange, migration wave, border dispute, and shared crisis contributes to a civilization's diplomatic memory.

---

# Scientific Basis

Throughout history, diplomacy has enabled civilizations to resolve disputes, establish alliances, expand trade, exchange knowledge, and maintain peace.

Diplomatic systems evolve alongside political institutions, economic interdependence, communication technologies, and cultural development.

International relationships are dynamic, adaptive, and deeply influenced by historical experience.

Diplomatic systems should summarize routine interactions while preserving historically significant agreements, conflicts, and cooperative achievements.

Historical memory should decay gradually but never disappear entirely for civilization-defining events.

Relationship calculations should derive from historical events rather than arbitrary modifiers.

# Closing Statement

Two kingdoms once fought for a mountain pass.

Thousands died.

Years later, a terrible winter struck them both.

One kingdom opened its granaries.

The other never forgot.

Generations passed.

The battlefield became a road.

The road became a marketplace.

The marketplace became a city where children from both kingdoms studied together.

In The Garden, diplomacy is not the absence of conflict.

It is the long memory that allows former enemies to become future partners.
