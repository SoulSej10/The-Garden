# Purpose

This document defines how emergent stories are presented to the player without interrupting, directing, or replacing natural exploration.

The Story System never forces stories upon the player.

It makes them discoverable.

Players choose how deeply they wish to engage with the world's history.

---

# Core Principle

**Stories should be found, not announced.**

The Garden avoids excessive notifications, mission pop-ups, or cinematic interruptions.

Instead, stories quietly reveal themselves through observation and exploration.

---

# Design Philosophy

The world does not pause to tell its story.

The player uncovers it.

A ruined castle tells a story.

A family tree tells another.

A timeline reveals another.

Every presentation should encourage curiosity rather than demand attention.

---

# Levels of Story Presentation

Stories may be encountered through:

Natural observation.

Citizen conversations.

Historical archives.

Maps.

Timelines.

Family records.

Civilization chronicles.

Monuments.

Ruins.

Artifacts.

No single interface should become the exclusive source of narrative.

---

# Passive Discovery

Players naturally notice stories through:

Changing landscapes.

Growing settlements.

Aging citizens.

Expanding kingdoms.

Environmental recovery.

Visible consequences.

The world itself becomes the first storyteller.

---

# Active Discovery

Players may deliberately investigate:

Historical events.

Important citizens.

Family histories.

Scientific discoveries.

Wars.

Trade routes.

Ancient ruins.

Lost civilizations.

The Story System supports investigation without prescribing it.

---

# Narrative Context

Every significant story should answer:

What happened?

Who participated?

Why did it happen?

What changed?

What happened afterward?

Context transforms isolated events into understandable narratives.

---

# Presentation Principles

Narrative presentation should always be:

Optional.

Non-intrusive.

Historically grounded.

Contextual.

Chronological.

Searchable.

Connected.

Player-driven.

Presentation should never interrupt simulation unless explicitly requested by the player.

---

# Story Connections

Stories naturally reference one another.

A scientist appears in:

Their biography.

A university's history.

Their civilization's timeline.

A scientific breakthrough.

Their family's genealogy.

Narratives become a connected historical web rather than isolated entries.

---

# Narrative Discovery

The Story System encourages questions.

Examples include:

Why did this city become wealthy?

Who built this monument?

Why is this forest protected?

Why did this civilization disappear?

The interface supports investigation rather than providing immediate answers.

# Closing Statement

A traveler stopped beside an ancient stone bridge.

There was no quest marker.

No glowing icon.

No narrator explained its importance.

Only a weathered inscription remained.

Curiosity led the traveler to an old archive.

The archive revealed a forgotten engineer.

The engineer's work connected two kingdoms.

Those kingdoms eventually united.

The bridge was never merely a bridge.

It was the beginning of a civilization.

The player discovered that story because they chose to ask a simple question.

In The Garden, the world never insists that you pay attention.

It simply rewards those who do.
