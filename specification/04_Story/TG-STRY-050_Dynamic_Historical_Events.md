# Purpose

This document defines how the Story System recognizes, evaluates, and records historically significant events emerging from the simulation.

The Story System never creates events.

It only observes them.

Its responsibility is to determine which events become part of the world's enduring narrative.

---

# Core Principle

**Events become historical because of their consequences, not their spectacle.**

A quiet scientific discovery may reshape civilization for centuries.

A massive battle may leave no lasting impact.

Historical importance is measured over time.

---

# Design Philosophy

The simulation produces countless events every day.

Most disappear.

Some influence nearby communities.

A few alter civilizations.

Only the rarest reshape the course of history.

The Story System discovers these differences through observation rather than predetermined rules.

---

# Event Recognition

Events may become historically significant through:

Long-term consequences.

Large-scale influence.

Intergenerational effects.

Institutional change.

Scientific breakthroughs.

Environmental transformation.

Cultural impact.

Economic disruption.

Political reorganization.

Recognition may occur immediately or centuries later.

---

# Event Scale

Historical events exist at multiple scales.

Personal.

Family.

Community.

Regional.

Civilizational.

Planetary.

Events may grow from one scale into another through accumulated consequences.

---

# Event Evolution

Historical events evolve.

For example:

A local drought.

↓

Regional migration.

↓

Border conflict.

↓

Formation of a kingdom.

↓

Beginning of an empire.

The Story System records the entire causal chain rather than isolated moments.

---

Scientific discoveries.

Natural disasters.

Wars.

Treaties.

Mass migrations.

Religious movements.

Economic crises.

Technological revolutions.

# Historical Importance

Importance is influenced by:

Duration.

Breadth of influence.

Depth of consequence.

Number of affected systems.

Historical persistence.

Narrative continuity.

Importance is never assigned based on visual spectacle alone.

---

# Cascading Events

One historical event may generate many others.

Examples include:

Discovery of agriculture.

↓

Population growth.

↓

Urbanization.

↓

Trade.

↓

Political centralization.

↓

Scientific advancement.

The Story System preserves these relationships.

---

# Event Retirement

Not every important event remains historically relevant forever.

Events may gradually become:

Forgotten.

Localized.

Overshadowed.

Reinterpreted.

Rediscovered.

Historical importance changes as civilizations evolve.

# Closing Statement

One spring, a beekeeper noticed fewer flowers than usual.

They planted wildflowers beside their fields.

Neighbors copied the idea.

Pollinators returned.

Harvests improved.

The village prospered.

Trade expanded.

A nearby city was founded.

Centuries later, historians celebrated the agricultural revolution that transformed the region.

No record mentioned the beekeeper.

The event itself was ordinary.

Its consequences were not.

In The Garden, history is rarely changed by dramatic moments.

It is changed by ordinary actions whose consequences refuse to end.
