# Purpose

This document defines how The Garden creates meaningful player experiences without relying on scripted quests, mandatory objectives, or predefined story progression.

Players are not guided through a narrative.

They discover one.

Their curiosity becomes the driving force behind exploration, observation, and intervention.

---

# Core Principle

**Curiosity replaces objectives.**

The Garden never asks:

> "Complete this mission."

Instead it invites the player to ask:

> "What is happening?"

> "Why did this happen?"

> "What might happen next?"

---

# Design Philosophy

The world does not wait for the player.

Civilizations grow.

Families expand.

Wars begin.

Forests recover.

Children are born.

People die.

Whether the player watches or not.

The player's experience comes from witnessing a living world rather than completing a sequence of assigned tasks.

---

# Player Motivation

Players naturally create personal goals.

Examples include:

Following one family for generations.

Protecting a struggling settlement.

Observing the rise of a civilization.

Studying animal migration.

Recording historical events.

Preserving endangered species.

Watching scientific progress.

Exploring unknown regions.

The simulation supports these goals without explicitly assigning them.

---

# Emergent Objectives

Objectives arise through observation.

For example:

A village is starving.

The player wonders why.

They discover repeated droughts.

They restore nearby forests.

The watershed recovers.

Agriculture improves.

The village survives.

The game never issued a quest.

The player created one.

---

# Intervention

Players may choose to intervene.

Or they may simply observe.

Neither choice is considered correct.

The Garden values curiosity as highly as action.

---

# Discovery

Meaningful discoveries include:

Hidden ecosystems.

Ancient ruins.

Forgotten trade routes.

Family histories.

Lost civilizations.

Scientific breakthroughs.

Unexpected migrations.

Rare natural events.

Discovery is rewarded with understanding rather than progression points.

---

# Progression

Player progression is measured through:

Knowledge.

Understanding.

Observation.

Perspective.

Historical awareness.

Emotional attachment.

The simulation does not require levels, experience points, or mandatory achievements to encourage continued play.

---

# Failure

There are no universal failure states.

Civilizations may collapse.

Species may disappear.

Wars may be lost.

History continues.

The player experiences consequences rather than game over screens.

---

# Replayability

Every simulation produces different questions.

Different civilizations.

Different histories.

Different people.

Different discoveries.

Players replay The Garden not to optimize outcomes, but to witness different worlds.

---

# State Variables

The Story System observes:

Player Discoveries.

Observed Narratives.

Personal Interests.

Historical Encounters.

Exploration Coverage.

Narrative Engagement.

These values describe the player's relationship with the world without directing their behavior.

# Closing Statement

A traveler noticed an abandoned road disappearing into the forest.

No marker appeared.

No objective was given.

No reward was promised.

Curiosity was enough.

The road led to the ruins of a forgotten city.

Its people had vanished centuries earlier.

Their writings described a disease long thought mythical.

That forgotten knowledge later saved another civilization from the same fate.

The traveler never completed a quest.

They simply chose to follow an old road.

In The Garden, the greatest adventures begin when nothing tells you where to go.
