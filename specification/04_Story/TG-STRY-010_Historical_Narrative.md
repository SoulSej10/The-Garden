# Purpose

This document defines how historical narratives emerge from the simulation and how they are recognized, connected, and presented over time.

A historical narrative is not written by the game.

It is reconstructed from causally related events that collectively explain how the world became what it is.

The Garden never invents history.

It observes it.

---

# Core Principle

**History is interpretation built upon causality.**

The simulation records events.

The Story System discovers relationships.

The Observatory presents those relationships.

---

# Design Philosophy

History is not a timeline.

It is a network of consequences.

A famine may trigger migration.

Migration may found a city.

The city may become a kingdom.

The kingdom may begin an empire.

Centuries later, historians call it "The Great Migration."

The people living through it never knew they were creating history.

---

# Narrative Chains

Historical narratives are constructed from connected events.

Examples include:

Discovery → Settlement → Expansion

Drought → Migration → Conflict

Innovation → Prosperity → Urbanization

War → Reform → Golden Age

Every chain is built from actual simulation events.

No links are fabricated.

---

# Historical Perspective

Different observers may describe the same history differently.

A victorious kingdom remembers liberation.

Its defeated rival remembers invasion.

A merchant remembers opportunity.

A soldier remembers loss.

The Garden allows multiple historical perspectives to coexist.

---

# Historical Scope

Narratives may exist at several scales.

Individual.

Family.

Settlement.

Region.

Civilization.

Planetary.

Smaller narratives often become chapters within larger historical movements.

---

# Turning Points

Certain events reshape future history disproportionately.

Examples include:

Founding of a kingdom.

Collapse of an empire.

Major scientific breakthrough.

Climate catastrophe.

Pandemic.

Religious reformation.

Discovery of a new continent.

Turning points emerge from their long-term consequences rather than immediate impact.

---

# Historical Themes

The Story System may recognize recurring themes such as:

Expansion.

Resilience.

Innovation.

Exploration.

Survival.

Unity.

Fragmentation.

Recovery.

These themes describe history rather than dictate it.

---

# Narrative Continuity

Historical narratives evolve continuously.

New discoveries may reshape the understanding of older events.

Previously insignificant actions may later become historically important.

History is continually reinterpreted as the world develops.

---

# Historical Uncertainty

Not every event is perfectly preserved.

Information may be:

Forgotten.

Distorted.

Lost.

Rediscovered.

Contradicted.

Incomplete histories create opportunities for future discovery.

---

# Narrative Significance

Historical significance increases when an event:

Produces lasting consequences.

Changes multiple systems.

Influences many individuals.

Persists across generations.

Creates new institutions.

Alters future decisions.

Significance is determined through impact, not spectacle.

# Closing Statement

A river changed course after decades of heavy rain.

Farmers relocated their fields.

A trading route shifted.

A small village grew into a bustling port.

Centuries later, historians praised the city's strategic brilliance.

Few remembered the river.

Yet it was the river that quietly rewrote the history of an entire civilization.

In The Garden, history is not the record of what happened.

It is the understanding of why the world became the way it is.
