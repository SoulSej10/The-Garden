# Purpose

This document defines how entire civilizations develop recognizable narratives throughout history.

Civilization stories are not written by the simulation.

They emerge from the accumulated actions, values, institutions, achievements, failures, and memories of generations.

A civilization's identity is therefore discovered rather than assigned.

---

# Core Principle

**A civilization is remembered for the patterns it repeats.**

One generation plants the seeds.

Another strengthens them.

Centuries later those patterns become identity.

---

# Design Philosophy

Civilizations are not characters.

They have no singular mind.

Their stories emerge from millions of individual decisions made across generations.

No citizen can fully control the civilization's future.

Yet every citizen contributes to it.

---

# Civilizational Identity

Every civilization gradually develops characteristics that distinguish it from others.

Identity may emerge through:

Scientific achievement.

Agricultural excellence.

Maritime exploration.

Trade.

Art.

Religion.

Military tradition.

Diplomacy.

Education.

Environmental adaptation.

Identity is descriptive rather than deterministic.

---

# Collective Memory

Civilizations remember through:

Education.

Traditions.

Architecture.

Laws.

Religion.

Language.

Monuments.

Historical records.

Collective memory influences future choices without dictating them.

---

# Shared Values

Long-term experiences shape cultural values.

Possible values include:

Innovation.

Honor.

Exploration.

Stability.

Independence.

Community.

Prosperity.

Scholarship.

Faith.

Resilience.

Values evolve gradually through historical experience.

# Civilizational Milestones

Major milestones include:

Founding.

Unification.

Expansion.

Golden ages.

Scientific revolutions.

Religious reformations.

Major migrations.

Economic transformation.

Collapse.

Recovery.

Each milestone becomes part of the civilization's narrative identity.

---

# Reputation

Civilizations gradually earn reputations.

Examples include:

Trusted trading partner.

Scientific leader.

Military power.

Cultural center.

Religious authority.

Neutral mediator.

Exploration pioneer.

Reputation emerges from observed historical behavior.

---

# Historical Transformation

Civilizations are capable of change.

A militaristic kingdom may later become a peaceful republic.

A trading nation may become an industrial power.

An isolated people may become global explorers.

Identity evolves continuously.

History never permanently defines a civilization.

---

# Internal Narratives

Different groups within the same civilization may remember history differently.

Examples include:

Urban and rural communities.

Different religions.

Political factions.

Regional cultures.

Social classes.

Civilization stories therefore contain multiple perspectives rather than a single official narrative.

---

# Narrative Recognition

The Story System identifies long-term patterns such as:

Centuries of peace.

Persistent innovation.

Repeated expansion.

Cultural resilience.

Economic leadership.

Environmental stewardship.

Historical significance emerges from accumulated evidence rather than isolated events.

# Closing Statement

A small settlement survived where larger kingdoms failed.

Not because it possessed stronger walls.

Not because it commanded larger armies.

But because every generation rebuilt after hardship instead of abandoning the land.

Centuries later, neighboring civilizations spoke of them with quiet admiration.

"They always endure."

No king ever declared endurance to be their national virtue.

History simply noticed that they never stopped rebuilding.

In The Garden, civilizations do not choose the stories told about them.

They become those stories one generation at a time.
