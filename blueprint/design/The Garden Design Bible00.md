# "A world that never sleeps."

---

# Purpose

This document defines the vision, philosophy, and identity of **The Garden**.

Every feature, line of code, simulation rule, UI decision, and future expansion must support the principles established here.

If a future feature contradicts this document, the feature must change—not the vision.

This document serves as the project's North Star.

---

# Executive Summary

The Garden is a persistent living world simulator.

It is not designed around winning, leveling up, completing quests, or maximizing efficiency.

Instead, it simulates a world that continuously evolves according to simple but interconnected systems.

Weather changes.

Forests spread.

Animals migrate.

People are born.

Families grow.

Villages rise.

Civilizations emerge.

Cultures evolve.

History is written.

The player is not the author of these events.

The player witnesses them.

The player influences them.

The player studies them.

The world ultimately belongs to itself.

---

# Mission Statement

Create a world capable of producing stories that were never explicitly written.

Every meaningful event should emerge naturally from interacting systems rather than handcrafted scripts.

Players should feel as though they are observing a real civilization instead of a collection of programmed mechanics.

---

# Vision Statement

The Garden seeks to become the most believable autonomous simulation possible—not through graphical realism, but through systemic realism.

The goal is not to convince players they are looking at reality.

The goal is to convince players that the world has a life of its own.

---

# Design Philosophy

Traditional games are collections of content.

The Garden is a collection of systems.

Traditional games ask:

"What should happen next?"

The Garden asks:

"What would naturally happen next?"

The distinction is fundamental.

Nothing should occur simply because a designer wanted it to.

Everything should occur because previous events made it possible.

---

# The Core Experience

The primary experience is observation.

Players arrive in an already-living world.

They witness:

* unexpected friendships
* failed harvests
* migrations
* discoveries
* celebrations
* disasters
* forgotten civilizations
* rebuilding after tragedy

The most memorable stories should not be written by developers.

They should emerge naturally from the simulation.

---

# The Player's Role

The player is not a hero.

The player is not a king.

The player is not "the chosen one."

Instead, the player acts as an observer, gardener, experimenter, historian, and occasional catalyst.

Players may influence conditions.

Players should rarely dictate outcomes.

The world must remain autonomous.

---

# The Meaning Behind the Name

A garden is alive.

It grows.

It changes.

It surprises.

It rewards patience rather than constant intervention.

Left alone, it continues becoming something new.

The project embraces this philosophy.

The Garden should feel cultivated rather than controlled.

---

# Project Identity

The Garden is not:

* a city builder
* a survival game
* a strategy game
* a sandbox toy
* an AI chatbot
* an idle game
* a colony simulator clone

The Garden is:

* a living ecosystem
* a historical simulation
* an emergent storytelling engine
* a persistent world
* a laboratory of interconnected systems

---

# The Three Pillars

## Pillar One

### Emergence Over Scripting

Whenever possible, replace scripted events with interacting systems.

Instead of writing:

"A famine begins."

Allow weather, agriculture, storage, trade, and population pressure to naturally create famine.

Emergence is always preferred.

---

## Pillar Two

### Persistence Over Sessions

The world should continue existing independent of observation.

Closing the application should never conceptually end the world's story.

The simulation has a past.

It has a present.

It has a future.

---

## Pillar Three

### History Over Gameplay

The Garden is fundamentally about history.

Every citizen contributes.

Every village leaves traces.

Every decision shapes future generations.

History is not background flavor.

History is one of the simulation's primary outputs.

Attachment

"I remember this family."

Reflection

"Everything changed because of that drought."

Loss

Discovery

"I've never seen the simulation do this before."

---

# Long-Term Vision

The Garden should become increasingly believable over time.

Each new system should deepen existing systems rather than replace them.

Examples include:

* ecology
* weather
* trade
* governance
* architecture
* education
* language
* myths
* religion
* technology
* exploration

These systems should interact instead of existing independently.

---

# Success Criteria

The project succeeds when players begin telling stories that developers never intentionally created.

Examples:

"I watched a family survive four generations."

"The kingdom collapsed because two rivers dried up."

"A forgotten village became a forest centuries later."

These stories should emerge naturally.

---

# Design Test

Before implementing any feature, ask:

Does this increase autonomy?

Does this deepen interaction between systems?

Can this create new stories?

Does it respect persistence?

If the answer is "no" to most of these questions, the feature should be reconsidered.

---

# Future Promise

The Garden is not a finished destination.

It is a living world that becomes richer with every new system.

Development should resemble cultivating an ecosystem rather than assembling a checklist.

The project will continue evolving long after its first playable version.

That evolution is not a side effect.

It is the project's purpose.
